Ethernet Standards

Cabled and Wireless

IEEE 802.11 is the standard that specifies connectivity for wireless networks. IEEE 802.11, or Wi-Fi, refers to a collective group of standards, as shown in Figure 1. These protocols specify the frequencies, speeds, and other capabilities of the different Wi-Fi standards.

802.11a – Allows WLANs to achieve data rates as high as 54 Mbps. Devices operate in the 5 GHz radio frequency range and within a maximum range of 45.7 m (150 ft).

802.11b – Operates in the 2.4 GHz frequency range with a maximum theoretical data rate of 11 Mbps. Devices operate within a maximum range of 91 m (300 ft).

802.11g – Provides the same theoretical maximum speed as 802.11a, which is 54 Mbps, but operates in the same 2.4 GHz spectrum as 802.11b. Unlike 802.11a, 802.11g is backward-compatible with 802.11b. 802.11g also has a maximum range of 91 m (300 ft).

802.11n – Has a theoretical bandwidth of 600 Mbps and operates in either the 2.4 GHz or 5 GHz frequency range with a maximum range of 250 m (984 ft). The 802.11n standard is backward-compatible with 802.11a, 802.11b, and 802.11g.
